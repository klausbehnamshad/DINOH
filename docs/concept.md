# Evaluating AI Support for Oral History

### A multilingual benchmark for the C²DH Oral History Workflow

---

## What this is

The C²DH Oral History Workflow uses computational tools to help researchers describe and structure recorded interviews. This project asks a simple but important question: when a tool proposes metadata or a thematic structure for an interview, how good are those proposals — and can we measure that in a way we trust?

To answer it, we built an evaluation dataset and a benchmark. The dataset is a *goldstandard*: a set of interview excerpts that a researcher has annotated by hand, segment by segment. The benchmark runs AI models over the same material and compares their output against the human reference, producing clear and repeatable scores.

## What it can do

The benchmark currently evaluates two tasks.

The first is **minimal metadata extraction**. Given an interview, can a model fill in a small set of descriptive fields — title, language, place, keywords, a short abstract — correctly? The second is **thematic segmentation**: can a model divide an interview into coherent thematic sections, placing the boundaries where a human reader would?

For each task we report established, well-understood measures. Metadata is scored by exact agreement with the human reference, field by field. Segmentation is scored with WindowDiff and Pk — two standard metrics from the research literature that capture how close a proposed set of boundaries is to the reference. Results are always broken down by language and never collapsed into a single average that would hide where a model struggles.

## What makes it different

Three commitments set this work apart from a generic AI evaluation.

**The reference is human.** The goldstandard is made by a researcher, not generated by another model. AI is limited to making suggestions; the final reading of a testimony remains a scholarly act. We do not ask the machine to interpret — only to assist — and we measure exactly that narrow contribution.

**Language is treated with care.** Oral history in Luxembourg is multilingual by nature, and some of its languages — Luxembourgish and Portuguese among them — are poorly served by mainstream tools. We report each language on its own terms and never pool scores across them, so that strong performance in English cannot mask weak performance in Luxembourgish.

**The benchmark knows its limits.** The deepest layer of interpretation — the overarching meaning a historian draws from a testimony — is deliberately left outside automated scoring. It belongs to the researcher. Fields that resist mechanical comparison are flagged for human review rather than scored by proxy.

## Data and transparency

All interview material in the current dataset is synthetic. The excerpts were written for methodological testing and represent no real person, testimony, or event; every record is marked accordingly. This lets us develop and publish the method openly, without exposing real testimonies.

The benchmark uses open-weight models, whose behaviour can be inspected and reproduced rather than taken on trust, and the descriptive fields follow a published minimal metadata schema. Every evaluation run is versioned and repeatable.

## Status

The project is at an early stage (version 1.1, draft). The goldstandard is currently annotated by a single researcher; a second annotator pass is planned, so that we can measure how far two trained readers agree. The evaluation has begun running on the university's high-performance computing infrastructure. We are sharing the concept now to invite discussion and refinement.

## Why it matters

Oral history rests on a careful relationship between a testimony and the person who interprets it. As computational tools enter this work, the question is not whether they are impressive, but whether they are transparent, measurable, and respectful of that relationship. This benchmark is our attempt to hold AI support to a clear and honest standard — one defined by researchers, for research.
